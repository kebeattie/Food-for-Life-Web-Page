Introduction

This page is an attempt to summarise the key diet advice from Tim Spector's 2022 book "Food for Life". The book draws on cutting edge research and personal insights from Professor Spector in an attempt to help us choose what to eat, and what to avoid. Subtitled "Your Guide to the New Science of Eating Well", the book goes in depth on a range of health and food related topics, including:

  • What is the microbiome?
  • Why do we love food?
  • What foods are really healthy/unhealthy?
  • How can foods effect us as unique individuals?
  • How do our food choices impact the enviroment?

Professor Spector also breaks down different types of foods and explains which we should seek/avoid.

Of course, if you're really interested in this, you should read the book for yourself! But if you don't have the time for a 400 page read, then I'll do my best to sum up Professor Spector's diet advice!

The Rainbow Diet

Polyphenols

Polyphenols are a vitally important group of plant chemicals that only our gut microbes can utilise. Essentially, polyphenols are plant chemicals created to protect against environmental or predator attacks. Foods vary massively in the quantities of polyphenols they contain, with different coloured vegetables of the same type varying up to tenfold! This is part of why it's important to vary the colours of the plants we eat. So why do we need polyphenols?

Polyphenols help protect your body by:

  • Improving heart health
  • Lowering diabetes risk
  • Raising immunity
  • Potentially lowering cancer risks

Vitamins and Minerals

Our body needs vitamins and minerals to perform properly and stay healthy. These vitamins and minerals are found in different foods in varying qualities, just like polyhenols, in foods of different colours. Of course, we all know about vitamins and minerals these days so I won't go into detail, but to make sure we get enough of them it's important to "eat the rainbow"!

Rainbow Diet Table

The following table shows Tim Spector's tips for vegetables and legumes catagorised by colour to increase vitamin, mineral, and polyphenol diversity in our diets.

Red Top Tips
Beans (kidney, red, adzuki) High in protein and beneficial polyphenols as well as fibre. Try to include beans in your meals every day.
Beetroot Rich in fibre, vitamins and polyphenols including anthocyanin, with as little sugar as an apple. Eat raw when thinly sliced or cooked (but avoid boiling the nutrients raw to death).
Bell peppers Higher in vitamin C than oranges. Eat raw in chopped salad for maximum vitamin C availability.
Chillies Great for antioxidants and vitamin A. Add to curries and soups.
Radish Slice into salads or sandwiches, ferment in kimchi or roast.
Tomato Cooked tomatoes are better than raw for their lycopene availability.
Orange
Carrot Best cooked for greater beta-carotene availability.
Pumpkin A squash variety that is overshadowed by Halloween but is packed with fibre and vitamin A. Delicious in stews, soups or curries. Tap on the side to see if it's edible: the denser it is the tastier.
Sweet potato (and purple potatoes) Great alternative to starchier white potatoes for a boost of polyphenols, ideally eaten with the skin on.
Squash A nutritious power-packed vegetable; works well in stews,soups or curries.
Yellow/brown
Cauliflower Save the nutritious leaves, steam or briefly roast them, and serve with extra vigin olive oil (EVOO) and a spritz of lemon.
Chickpeas Fantastic source of fibre and protein and shown to reduce blood glucose in trials. Best eaten whole in salds, soups and curries.
Corn High in lutein and zeaxanthin, which may help maintain eye heatlh (based on trials).
Garlic Packed with prebiotic fibre inulin, which benefits the gut microbiome; excellent anti-inflammatory properties.
Mushrooms Eat them everyday for a great micronutrient boost, and a source of protein and vitamin D.
Parsnips Contains starch so they are energy-dense and contribute to fibre diversity.
Potato Always leave the skin on. Contains more potassium than a banana and more fibre than an apple; high in vitamin C and a good source of iron.
Soybeans A small snack of edamane gies you areound 11 grams of protein and 8 grams of fibre. Enjoy in tofu form as a meat alternative.
Green
Artichoke High in the prebiotic fibre inulin to feed our gut microbiome. Delicious fresh but also when jarred or frozen.
Asparagus Asparagus is one of the few exceptions to the polyphenol colour rule - green and white varieties have more polyphenols than than purple assparagus.
Avocado Excellent source of monounsaturated fats, fibre, vitmains and polyphenols.
Broad beans Nourishing and versatile food frequently used in European and Middle Eastern cuisine but underused in the UK. A good source of fibre and protein and helps make meals more filling.
Broccoli Try a range of broccoli varieties including purple. Can be eaten raw or cooked; contains iron as well as beneficial fibres.
Brussel sprouts Roast them or steam them lightly to avoid the foul smell of boiled sprouts. A great seasonal vegetable packed with fibre.
Cabbage Steam for less than 5 minutes to retain nutrients and avoid the rotten egg smell. Ferment to feed your gut.
Celery When slowly braised it greatly enhances over flavours in tomatoes, olive oil and onions.
Cucumber A good snack with hummus and counts as one of your plants, but otherwise nothing else special.
Iceberg Lettuce Its only good point is that it lasts for weeks in your refrigerator. Tim's least favourite plant!
Lentils High in protein, iron, fibre and nutrients, they are higher in ition gram-for-gram than steak or chicken. Cook from dried form to bring out their complex flavours.
Peas Maintain structure well when froze - keep a bag in the freezer to cheaply top up your meals.
Rocket and other leaves The darker or redder the leaf the higher the polyphenols.
Spinach Keep a bag of this leaf in your freezer for an affordable, nutritious boost to meals.
